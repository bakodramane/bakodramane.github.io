A large share of the world's agriculture happens on holdings that no survey reliably reaches, in countries whose statistical systems were never resourced to measure them year after year. The 50x2030 Initiative to Close the Agricultural Data Gap was set up to change that: to help partner countries build integrated, survey-based agricultural data systems that can monitor Sustainable Development Goal 2 and inform national policy. As the agency leading the data production component, FAO supports countries in collecting the data that the Goal's indicators require, particularly 2.3.1 and 2.3.2 on the productivity and income of small-scale food producers, and 2.4.1 on sustainable agriculture.

Having helped design the sampling approach for the Initiative and worked with national teams putting it into practice, I want to set out the lessons that have held up across very different country settings.

## Lesson one: build the frame from the census

An integrated survey programme is only as sound as the frame it samples from. The most durable arrangement uses the agricultural census as the backbone: the census provides complete structural coverage once a decade, and the inter-censal surveys draw their samples from it and focus on the fast-changing items the census deliberately leaves out. This is the same logic that runs through the WCA 2030 guidelines, and it is why the census and survey programmes are best designed as one system rather than two.

The household sector is the straightforward part. The complication is the non-household sector: corporations, government and cooperative holdings, and the large commercial farms that sit awkwardly between the two. These are few in number but heavy in production, so missing them distorts national totals. A dual-frame design, drawing the non-household holdings from a business or farm register and the household holdings from the census-based area or list frame, is usually the right answer, with clear rules for classifying the ambiguous cases so that a holding is counted once and only once.

## Lesson two: settle the sampling design before the questionnaire

It is tempting to start with the questionnaire, because content feels concrete. The more reliable sequence settles the sampling design first, because the design determines what the survey can actually estimate. The 50x2030 [*Guide to Sampling*](https://www.50x2030.org/resources/document-library) was written to give national teams a defensible starting point: stratification that reflects the structure of the sector, sample sizes tied to the precision the indicators demand, and a rotation design that lets a panel of holdings carry over between years while refreshing part of the sample.

Rotation deserves emphasis. A pure cross-section each year cannot measure change at the holding level, and a fixed panel decays and fatigues. A rotation design balances the two, and it is one of the recurring methodological threads across my work on repeated agricultural surveys.

## Lesson three: design the questionnaire around the indicators you must report

Working backwards from the indicator set keeps the questionnaire disciplined. Indicator 2.4.1, on the area under productive and sustainable agriculture, is demanding because it bundles together environmental, economic, and social sub-criteria, and each sub-criterion has to be operationalised into questions a respondent can answer. Indicators 2.3.1 and 2.3.2, on volume of production per labour unit and on income, depend on measuring output and labour well, which is exactly where measurement error tends to concentrate. Where the budget allows, objective methods such as crop-cutting for yield, and rotating economic modules for income, raise the quality of the hardest variables.

## Lesson four: plan the analytical outputs alongside the data collection

A survey that produces clean micro-data and stops there has done half its job. The data earns its keep when it reaches the people making agricultural policy, in a form they can use. Earlier this year I worked with the Ministry of Agriculture of Burkina Faso on a training workshop, run under the IFAD-supported strand of the Initiative, focused specifically on turning survey results into analytical and policy briefs. The lesson from that work is simple and easy to neglect: build the tabulation plan and the brief templates while the survey is still in the field, so that the analytical pipeline is ready when the data arrives rather than improvised afterwards.

## Country experience across the region

The Initiative and its predecessor programmes have run across a wide range of settings, and the West and Central African experience is instructive. Sierra Leone's National Agriculture Sample Survey shows how a country can stand up a recurring survey programme and report on it at the regional level. Burkina Faso's work on policy briefs shows the downstream half of the chain, where data becomes decision support.

## Sequencing the work

For a national team starting out, the order that has served well is this. Build the frame from the most recent census. Settle the sampling design, including the rotation, before drafting content. Choose the indicators you are obliged to report and design the questionnaire around them. Plan the tabulation and the policy outputs in parallel with the fieldwork. None of these steps is glamorous, and getting the order right is most of what separates a survey programme a country can sustain from one that stalls after the first cycle.
